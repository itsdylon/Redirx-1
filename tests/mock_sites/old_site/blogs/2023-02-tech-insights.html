Technology Insights for Modern Enterprises

Published: February 10, 2023 | Author: David Nakamura, CTO

Technology is evolving faster than ever, creating both unprecedented opportunities and significant challenges for enterprise IT leaders. Drawing on our experience helping organizations navigate digital transformation, here are key insights we believe every CTO and IT leader should consider when planning technology strategy.

Build vs. Buy: A Nuanced Decision

The build versus buy decision isn't binary, and the right answer has evolved significantly in recent years. The best approach often involves a hybrid strategy: buy commodity capabilities where differentiation doesn't matter, build where you need unique competitive advantages.

When evaluating options, consider total cost of ownership including maintenance, upgrades, and opportunity costs—not just initial acquisition or development costs. A commercial solution might have higher upfront costs but lower long-term expenses. Custom development provides control but creates ongoing maintenance obligations.

We're seeing successful organizations adopt a platform approach: buy best-of-breed components and integrate them with custom code that delivers unique business value. This balances speed to market with differentiation.

Technical Debt is Business Debt

Technical debt—shortcuts taken during development that create future work—directly impacts business agility and competitiveness. Legacy systems, outdated dependencies, and accumulating patches slow innovation, increase risk, and make attracting talent harder.

Organizations should treat tech debt reduction as a business priority, not just an IT concern. Allocate 15-20% of development capacity to addressing technical debt systematically. The cost of deferring this work compounds over time, eventually consuming resources that could drive innovation.

Make technical debt visible to business stakeholders by translating it into business terms: slower feature delivery, higher risk of outages, difficulty scaling. This builds support for the investment needed to address it.

Platform Thinking

Leading organizations are shifting from building individual applications to creating platforms that enable rapid development of new capabilities. This requires upfront investment in APIs, shared services, developer tools, and governance, but the payoff is exponentially faster innovation.

Platform thinking means building reusable components, establishing clear interfaces, and creating self-service capabilities for developers. It's about maximizing leverage—write once, use many times. Companies like Amazon, Netflix, and Spotify have demonstrated the power of this approach.

Start by identifying common capabilities across applications. Build these as shared services with well-documented APIs. Create developer portals, sample code, and tooling that makes using platform services easier than building from scratch.

Data as a Strategic Asset

Data is only valuable if it's accessible, trustworthy, and actionable. Too many organizations have data locked in silos, of questionable quality, or so difficult to access that insights come too late to matter.

Invest in data governance to establish clear ownership, quality standards, and access policies. Build data catalogues so people can find what they need. Create self-service analytics capabilities so business users can answer their own questions without always depending on IT or data teams.

Modern data architectures like data mesh and data fabric offer new approaches to these challenges. The key is making data easily discoverable and usable while maintaining appropriate controls and quality.

Security by Design

Security cannot be an afterthought or something handled solely by a specialized team. It must be embedded into every stage of development through DevSecOps practices that make security everyone's responsibility.

Automate security testing and integrate it into CI/CD pipelines. Use infrastructure as code to ensure consistent, secure configurations. Implement continuous compliance monitoring to detect drift from security baselines. Make security findings visible and actionable for development teams.

This shift requires cultural change as much as technical change. Developers need security training, security teams need to enable rather than just gate keep, and executives must support security investments even when they slow feature delivery.

Invest in Your People

Technology is only as good as the people who conceive, build, and use it. In a world where technical talent is scarce and expensive, investing heavily in training, creating clear career paths, and fostering cultures of continuous learning isn't optional—it's strategic.

The war for technical talent makes retention as important as recruitment. Provide opportunities for growth, expose team members to cutting-edge technologies, and create environments where talented people want to work. This means supporting professional development, allowing time for learning, and building inclusive cultures.

Don't forget the business users who ultimately determine whether technology investments succeed. Invest in their digital literacy, involve them early in design, and create feedback loops that ensure technology serves real needs.

Conclusion

These insights reflect patterns we see across successful digital transformations. They're not revolutionary ideas, but their consistent application separates organizations that thrive from those that struggle with technology.

The common thread is taking a strategic, long-term view of technology rather than treating it as a tactical necessity. Organizations that succeed view technology as a core capability requiring continuous investment in tools, processes, and people.
